DETECTIVE ACADEMY

Learn SQL through investigation. Master the art of querying databases to uncover hidden truths. Your training begins now.

1

Understand the Case

Every investigation begins with understanding what happened. Read the case briefing carefully. Note the victim, location, time of death, and any initial evidence provided. This context will guide your investigation and help you form hypotheses about what queries to run.

In Project Vritra, each case has a briefing page that provides all the background information you need. Take notes. Ask questions. The more you understand the situation, the more effective your SQL queries will be.

Detective Tip

  • Pay attention to dates and times—they're crucial for timeline analysis
  • Look for connections between the victim and potential suspects
  • Note any unusual patterns or inconsistencies in the evidence
2

Explore the Database

Every case provides access to a database containing relevant information: witnesses, suspects, evidence, financial records, surveillance data, and more. Your first task is to understand what tables are available and what information each contains.

Start with simple queries to explore the data structure. Use SELECT statements to see what columns exist in each table. This exploration phase is crucial—knowing your data is half the battle.

Example: Explore a table
SELECT * FROM witnesses;

Detective Tip

  • Always start by understanding table structure before writing complex queries
  • Look for foreign keys that connect different tables
  • Note data types—they'll affect how you filter and join data
3

Filter with WHERE Clauses

Once you understand the data, you need to narrow it down to relevant information. The WHERE clause is your primary tool for filtering data based on conditions. Think of it as asking specific questions about the evidence.

For example, if you need to find witnesses who saw the victim in the 24 hours before death, you'd use a WHERE clause to filter by time. If you're looking for suspects with a specific connection to the victim, filter by that relationship.

Example: Find witnesses from a specific time
SELECT * FROM witnesses
WHERE sighting_time >= '2024-01-15 00:00:00'
AND sighting_time <= '2024-01-15 23:59:59';

Detective Tip

  • Use AND to combine multiple conditions
  • Use OR for alternative conditions
  • Be precise with your filters—too broad and you'll get irrelevant data, too narrow and you might miss important clues
4

Connect with JOINs

The most powerful insights come from connecting different pieces of evidence. SQL JOINs allow you to combine data from multiple tables based on related columns. This is how you find connections that aren't obvious in any single dataset.

For example, you might join witness statements with suspect profiles to find overlaps. Or join financial transactions with surveillance data to correlate money movements with physical presence.

Example: Connect witnesses to suspects
SELECT w.name, s.suspect_name, w.statement
FROM witnesses w
INNER JOIN suspects s
ON w.location_id = s.location_id;

Detective Tip

  • INNER JOIN returns only matching records from both tables
  • LEFT JOIN returns all records from the left table, even if no match exists
  • Always verify your join logic—wrong joins can lead to incorrect conclusions
5

Aggregate with GROUP BY

Sometimes the truth lies in patterns across multiple records. SQL's aggregate functions (COUNT, SUM, AVG, MAX, MIN) combined with GROUP BY allow you to analyze data at a higher level, finding trends and anomalies that individual records might hide.

For example, you might count how many times each suspect appears in witness statements, or sum financial transactions to find unusually large payments.

Example: Count witness mentions per suspect
SELECT s.suspect_name, COUNT(w.id) AS mention_count
FROM suspects s
LEFT JOIN witnesses w ON s.id = w.suspect_id
GROUP BY s.suspect_name
ORDER BY mention_count DESC;

Detective Tip

  • Use COUNT to find frequencies and patterns
  • Use SUM for financial analysis
  • Use MAX/MIN to find extremes in time, amounts, or other numeric values
6

Analyze Results

Running queries is only the beginning. The real detective work happens when you analyze the results. Look for patterns, anomalies, connections, and contradictions. Each query result is a piece of evidence—how does it fit with what you already know?

Ask yourself: Does this result confirm or contradict my hypothesis? Does it reveal new questions? Does it connect to other evidence? The best detectives don't just find data—they understand what it means.

Detective Tip

  • Look for outliers—data points that don't fit the pattern
  • Compare results across different time periods
  • Correlate findings with other evidence sources
  • Document your findings and the queries that produced them
7

Solve the Mystery

Each case has multiple objectives. Complete them all to unlock the final truth. Objectives guide your investigation, teaching you progressively more complex SQL concepts while advancing the story. As you complete objectives, you'll unlock clues and evidence that bring you closer to solving the case.

The final objective in each case requires you to identify the killer and their motive. This is where all your SQL skills and detective intuition come together. Use everything you've learned—filtering, joining, aggregating, and analysis—to build your case and make your accusation.

Detective Tip

  • Complete objectives in order—they're designed to teach progressively
  • Use hints if you're stuck, but they cost points
  • Review all unlocked clues before making your final accusation
  • Each solved case unlocks the next in the story
